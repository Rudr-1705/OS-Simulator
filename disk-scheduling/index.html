Disk Scheduling Overview

Disk scheduling algorithms determine the order in which pending disk I/O requests are serviced. Since seek time is the largest component of disk access time, the main objective of disk scheduling is to reduce total head movement and improve overall disk throughput.

Disk access time mainly consists of seek time, rotational latency and transfer time. Seek time is the time required to move the disk arm to the desired cylinder, while rotational latency is the waiting time for the required sector to rotate beneath the read/write head.

FCFS
Services requests in arrival order. Fair and simple, but may result in large seek movements.
SSTF
Selects the request nearest to the current head position. Reduces seek time but may cause starvation.
SCAN / LOOK
Head moves in one direction servicing requests before reversing. LOOK stops at the last request instead of the disk boundary.
C-SCAN / C-LOOK
Services requests only in one direction, giving more uniform waiting time across requests.

Performance Notes

Algorithm Seek Time Starvation Fairness
FCFS High No High
SSTF Low Possible Medium
SCAN Medium Low Better than SSTF
C-SCAN Medium Low Uniform wait time
LOOK Lower than SCAN Low Good
C-LOOK Lower than C-SCAN Low Uniform wait time

SCAN-based algorithms are commonly preferred in operating systems because they provide a balance between low seek time and fairness. Circular variants further improve fairness by providing nearly uniform waiting time to all requests.
